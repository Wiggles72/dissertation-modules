The Job Opportunity

Learn to spot online job scams before they go too far.

By the end of this scenario, you will be able to:

  • ✓ Spot when photo requests are a red flag
  • ✓ Know when to ask a trusted adult for help
  • ✓ Name one thing you can do to stay safe
📖 The Situation

Meet Alex

Alex is 17 and has been staying at a drop-in center for three weeks. Their phone plan is about to run out. While scrolling through social media, Alex sees a post:

"Brand Ambassador — $100/day, easy work from home. DM for details!"

Alex needs money. This looks like a way to get it fast. Alex sends a message.

But things are not what they seem.

⚠️ How It Escalates

How This Kind of Scam Works

These scams move fast. Here is what the pattern usually looks like:

Days 1–2
💬 First Contact "We need photos to verify you — headshot and full body."
Days 3–4
📸 More Photos "Now we need lifestyle shots — gym selfies, mirror photos."
Days 4–5
⚠️ Pressure Language "Prove you're serious." / "These are for internal use only."
Day 6+
🔒 Blackmail "Send money or I share these photos with everyone."
💬 Days 1–2

The First Messages

Hi Alex! Thanks for reaching out. We work with lifestyle brands and pay people to post content. Can I ask a few questions?
11:32 AM
Sure. What kind of work is it?
11:35 AM
Fitness, tech, and fashion brands. You post content and engage with followers. $100 per post. Interested?
Yeah, for sure. What do I need to do?
11:40 AM
First, we need verification photos — a headshot and a full-body photo. Just to confirm your identity.
🚩 Red Flag #1 — This Is Moving Fast

A real job does not ask for photos before:

  • Telling you the contract details
  • Explaining how you get paid
  • Giving you a company name you can look up

The professional language sounds real. But the request is not normal.

💬 Days 3–4

The Requests Get Bigger

I can send photos. What should I wear?
Just something casual! Natural is best.
Next day
Great photos! Now we need lifestyle shots to set you apart — gym selfies, mirror photos in casual clothes. These are for our internal portfolio only. This shows us you are serious about the job.
🚩 Red Flag #2 — Notice the Change

The requests keep getting more specific:

  • Casual photos → gym photos → mirror selfies
  • "Prove you're serious" is pressure language, not a job requirement
  • "Internal use only" is meant to stop you from questioning it
This is a grooming tactic. The next ask will be for more revealing photos. Real employers do not work this way.
🤔 Your Choice

What Should Alex Do?

Alex wants the money. But something feels off. What is the right move?
⚠️ This leads to a trap

Once Alex sends more photos, the scammer has what they need to make threats. The "job" disappears and the blackmail begins. Sending photos does not get you the job — it gives them power over you.

⚠️ Some protection — but not enough

Blocking stops this person from contacting you. But you are still alone with what happened. A trusted adult can help you report it and make sure you are safe going forward.

✅ Safe and supported

Alex's case manager recognizes the pattern right away. They help block the account, report it, and connect Alex to real job opportunities. Asking for help was the right call.

💡 Key Takeaways

What to Remember

🚩 Red Flags to Watch For

  • 📸Photo requests that keep getting more specific
  • ❓Vague job description with no company name
  • 💬"Prove you're serious" or "internal use only"
  • 🔒Pressure to keep the conversation private

✅ What You Can Do

  • 👤Tell a case manager, counselor, or shelter staff
  • 🔍Search the account name online to check for warnings
  • ❌Block the account and stop responding
  • 📋Take screenshots before you block

💭 Remember This

  • ⚡Needing money is real. Scammers know this and use it.
  • 🛡️Asking for help is a strong move, not a weak one.
  • ❤️Your case manager has seen this before. They will not judge you.
  • 🎯Real, safe jobs exist. Staff can help you find them.
